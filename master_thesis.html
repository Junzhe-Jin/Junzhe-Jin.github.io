Dynamic Crossmodal Mappings

A Visual and Haptic Interface with Shape-Changing and Colour-Shifting Materials.

Background & Motivation

Crossmodal mappings—the natural association between distinct sensory modalities—play a crucial role in enhancing multisensory interactive systems. While existing HCI research has extensively investigated dynamic mappings of physical transformation (e.g., shape-change) to implicit concepts like emotion or sound, or mapped static properties like tactile texture to color, a significant research gap remains. The corresponding relationship between **dynamic physical shape-change** and **visual color dimensions** has been largely unexplored. This master thesis project addresses this gap by creating and evaluating a novel interactive interface that enables this specific, under-researched crossmodal mapping.

System Design & Implementation

The project focuses on developing a visual and haptic interface leveraging soft robotics and responsive materials. We designed a pneumatically actuated soft shape-changing device fabricated using silicone and custom transparent pigments. This system allows for precise control over physical transformation dimensions, such as degree of expansion or surface curvature. Simultaneously, we integrate a programmable color-shifting mechanism that provides synchronous visual feedback. This cohesive setup allows us to computationally link the dimensions of physical deformation with specific visual color properties (e.g., color saturation or brightness) in real-time.

HCI Contributions & Research Value

The primary contribution of this thesis is expanding the theoretical and practical framework of haptic and tangible interaction within HCI. It bridges the subfields of soft robotics, tangible user interfaces (TUIs), and multisensory perception. By decoupling spatial navigation from overloaded senses (like vision) or creating intuitive, embodied feedback, this work introduces new possibilities for non-visual communication and inclusive design. The research provides a foundational methodology for future HCI researchers and designers to create more intuitive, expressive, and multisensory technologies by leveraging the natural crossmodal connection between dynamic form and color.

"To the best of my knowledge, this is the first study to investigate the crossmodal dynamic mappings between dynamic physical transformation dimensions and colour, providing unique contributions to tangible interaction research."
